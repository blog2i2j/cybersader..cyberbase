> [!tldr] Building a framework for organizing is better than building the structure itself first

> I'm trying to build a "Knowledge Space/Platform Organization Framework". The goal here is to give a consistent framework that will work on most knowledge platforms (like Obsidian, Notion, other "second brain" platforms.

> My goal is to build a framework likely to represent the input, work, and output of the knowledge platform. This should help build the structures typically found in knowledge platforms to improve the three purposes of the knowledge platform and empower and orient the system towards those purposes.

> This is the hardest part of figuring our knowledge platforms.  Here's my take on it.

# Organizing Your Second Brain from First Principles

## WO(R)C(S) Overview (potential framework acronym)

WORCS is similar to Nick Milo's ARC approach to personal knowledge management (PKM).  However, it's a simple idea that extends everywhere.  We want to make a good knowledge platform workflow and system, so we break it down by inputs, function, and outputs.  This is how engineers approach things and it's applicable here.  

I surmise that we can use the components of WORCS, COW, or COWS to organize the organizational components of our knowledge platforms (like Obsidian).  This is a first principles approach to maximizing the utility of these systems - no matter the context.  

We use WORCS to breakdown this framework into 5 potential components with 3 core components.  WORCS refers to knowledge WORK, knowledge OUTPUT, knowledge RELATION (not as important or "first principally"), knowledge CAPTURE, and knowledge SYSTEM (the system or context of the knowledge platform).  We can apply WORCS with rules to define how structure can emerge from our knowledge management system (KMS).  I'm still not sure that the RELATION part is fundamental enough to include.

## Potential Framework Breakdowns

### 1st Layer: Framework Conceptual Components

Below are ideas I have for the framework (I'm not sure which to go with).  It matters because I'm trying to take a first principles approach here.

#### A(CWO)E - activities (capture, work, output), entity

- Activities / Purpose / Interests / Intention / Goal of Using Knowledge Platform
	- Knowledge CAPTURE, INPUT
	- Knowledge WORK, PROCESSING
	- Knowledge OUTPUT, COMMUNICATION, PUBLISHING
- Interfacing ENTITY/AUDIENCE/Knowledge WORKER/AGENT (depending on context)

#### WO(R)C(S) - work, output, relation, capture, system (OR COW - capture, output, work)

- knowledge WORK - knowledge work is where you derive utility from utilizing the system/platform in combination with the knowledge towards some goal
- knowledge OUTPUT - knowledge output refers to designing the platform or system in a way where knowledge can be retrieved by certain entities or outputting to other systems or entities such as outputting or exporting documents
- knowledge RELATION - knowledge relation isn't necessarily a core activity of a knowledge platform.  This is why I put it in parentheses.  One could argue that RELATION is just part of knowledge work.  However, maybe you could have structures like (still not sure relate is as important as work or action or focusing on purpose)folders or tags specifically designed for bridging gaps between different activities in your knowledge platform.  It's debatable.
- knowledge CAPTURE - knowledge capture refers to how you get knowledge quickly into the platform.
- knowledge SYSTEM - refers to the knowledge platform or system itself which affects how all components are utilized (Obsidian or Notion would be examples of systems.

### 2nd Layer: Framework Application/Implementation

This is the harder part.  You have realize the implementation or design of the knowledge platform/system to use the framework.

There are two pieces:
- Understand the capabilities of the knowledge platform and how they can fulfill your goals for applicable conceptual components.
- Define rules on the knowledge system/platform that involve the conceptual framework components

### Isn't PARA enough?

- The thing about PARA is that it may cover all of ones needs.  However, it usually doesn't because they need a place for OUTPUTS (such as a root-level taxonomy or library-like catalogue or structure) or PARA is too action-oriented maybe doesn't work for some case.  A knowledge work space may have a strict taxonomy or a project-oriented structure like PARA.  The important part is that you have something that can scale based on the activities, purposes, and needs of the system and entities using it.

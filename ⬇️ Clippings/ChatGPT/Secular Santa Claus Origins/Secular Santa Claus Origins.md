**Short answer up-front:** No single person “invented” the idea of fibbing to children about a jolly, secular Santa. Instead, several 19th-century New York writers and artists—most notably **Washington Irving**, **Clement Clarke Moore**, and **Thomas Nast**—gradually recast the medieval, religious Saint Nicholas into a purely domestic, child-focused figure who visits on Christmas Eve. Their stories and images encouraged parents to stage an elaborate surprise for their youngsters. By the time the famous 1897 editorial “Yes, Virginia, there is a Santa Claus” appeared, the custom of *knowingly deceiving* children was already mainstream. Later mass-marketing (especially Coca-Cola’s 1930s ads) froze the look and further stripped away the saintly roots, cementing today’s fully secular Santa.

## 1\. From Bishop to Folk Figure: Pre-19th-Century Threads

- **St Nicholas of Myra** (4th c.) was revered for secret gift-giving; medieval Europeans kept his *feast* on 6 Dec.
- **Reformation shift:** Martin Luther tried to replace Nicholas with the *Christkindl* so that gifts pointed to Christ, but in Lutheran regions a *Weihnachtsmann* (Christmas Man) soon re-secularized the role.
- **Dutch Sinterklaas**—a tall, bishop-like figure—boarded ships to New Amsterdam in the 17th c.; yet his feast stayed religious and occurred on 5 Dec., not Christmas.

## 2\. The New-York Makeover (1809-1860s)

### 2.1 Washington Irving & John Pintard

- In *A History of New-York* (1809), Irving whimsically pictured St Nicholas flying over rooftops in a wagon, sowing the first seeds of a magical, gift-dropping Santa detached from liturgy.
- Antiquarian John Pintard promoted Nicholas as a civic symbol for the New-York Historical Society (1810), further secularizing him for city pride.

### 2.2 Clement Clarke Moore’s Poem (1823)

- *“A Visit from St. Nicholas”* (“’Twas the Night Before Christmas”) reset the visit to **Christmas Eve**, shrank the saint into a “right jolly old elf,” supplied reindeer, and most importantly **addressed children directly**, prompting parents to conspire so youngsters would awaken to proofs of his midnight call.

## 3\. Visual Standardization: Thomas Nast

- Political cartoonist **Thomas Nast** drew the first rotund, bearded Santa in *Harper’s Weekly* (1863). His 33 subsequent illustrations (1863-1886) placed Santa at the North Pole and equipped him with lists of children, rooting the idea that he is watching kids and judging behavior—another lever that encourages parental “myth-maintenance.”

## 4\. Mass-Market Santa: Coca-Cola & the 20th Century

- Haddon Sundblom’s Coca-Cola ads (starting 1931) fixed the red-and-white suit and genial face known worldwide, fully detaching Santa from ecclesiastical garb and amplifying the commercial, family-room focus.

## 5\. When Did Parents Start **Lying** About Him?

| Milestone | Evidence of Adult-Led Deception |
| --- | --- |
| 1820s-1840s | Newspapers describe children hanging stockings and waiting for *Moore’s* Santa—implying parents played along. |
| 1860s | Nast cartoons show Santa checking lists “of naughty and nice” children, encouraging parents to leverage belief for behavior. |
| 1897 | *New York Sun* answers eight-year-old Virginia O’Hanlon; the very question presumes adults have fostered belief. |

By the late 19th century, moralists were already debating the ethics of this “paternalistic lie.” Newspapers, magazines, and eventually psychologists weighed the trust costs versus imaginative benefits—discussions still alive today.

## 6\. Why This Matters

- **Cultural engine:** The secular Santa popularized gift-centred, child-centred Christmas observances, even revitalizing holiday attendance at Sunday schools—an irony noted by church historians.
- **Ethical debate:** Modern philosophers label the custom a “widespread deception” that can undermine trust, though some claim it nurtures imagination and critical thinking once children discern the truth.

---

### Bottom line

The *practice* of parents pretending Santa is literally real can be traced to Moore’s 1823 poem—shaped visually by Nast and commercialized by Coca-Cola—rather than to the medieval saint himself. Those 19th-century storytellers **didn’t set out to invent a lie**, but their charming secular Santa made parental deception *nearly inevitable* as the tradition spread.

## 1 What Counts as a Lie?

Aquinas defines a lie as three elements combined: *false matter*, *the will to state the false*, and *the intent to deceive*—all present in the Santa ruse. Kant and the Markkula Center likewise insist that deceiving another person attacks their rational autonomy, making lying intrinsically wrong whatever the consequences.

---

## 2 Non-Utilitarian Judgments

### 2.1 Augustine & Aquinas (Theological Realism)

Both church fathers reject even “benevolent” fibs: Augustine’s *De Mendacio* says every lie is sin because it disorders the soul, while Aquinas’s *Summa* classifies lying among the vices opposed to truth.

### 2.2 Kantian Deontology

Kant argues that any exception to truth-telling would undermine the very possibility of reliable speech; thus lying to spare feelings (or to enchant children) fails the categorical imperative.

### 2.3 Virtue Ethics

Virtue theorists measure acts by the character they cultivate. Sustained parental deception habituates children—and parents themselves—to *practical dishonesty*, eroding the virtue of honesty even if no immediate harm is obvious.

## 4 What the Data Say About Long-Term Effects

- **Trust:** Some children do feel betrayed; psychologists Boyle & McKay caution that the lie can “undermine children’s trust in their parents.”
- **Credulity:** Philosopher David Kyle Johnson lists degraded parental credibility and encouraged gullibility among the main harms.
- **Critical Thinking:** Studies find that belief fades when children begin applying physical-possibility reasoning, but heavy parental promotion delays this cognitive milestone.

Even where empirical work reports *limited* damage, it cannot establish that the practice is morally acceptable—only that many children cope with adult wrongdoing.

## 5 Living the Truth Without Killing Wonder

1. **Reframe Santa as a shared game:** Tell children the story up-front as participatory fantasy (“We *pretend* Santa together”). Imagination flourishes without deceit.

3. **Celebrate Saint Nicholas historically:** Teach the real 4th-century bishop’s secret charity, preserving inspiration without falsehood.
4. **Cultivate traditions of truth:** Advent crafts, giving trees, and Nativity plays foster anticipation and wonder grounded in reality.

These approaches satisfy the deontological demand for honesty and the virtue-ethical pursuit of integrity while still providing the holiday magic utilitarians care about—achieving both short-term joy *and* long-term moral goods.
